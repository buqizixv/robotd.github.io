Mitsubishi Electric Bets Big on Physical AI, Partners With Chiba Institute to Build Japan's Homegrown Robot Brain

Published May 26, 2026

Japan is making its most decisive move yet into the Physical AI arena. Mitsubishi Electric, the ¥5.9 trillion industrial giant, and Chiba Institute of Technology have signed a three-year basic agreement to co-research and develop homegrown Physical AI technologies — a direct signal that Japan intends to compete with American and Chinese players in the race to build the robot brain of the future.

The Partnership

Under the agreement, which runs through April 2029, the two organizations will establish a dedicated co-creation center focused on commercializing AI-powered robotics solutions. The scope is deliberately broad: multi-legged walking robots for disaster response and infrastructure inspection, humanoid robots for service and manufacturing, and autonomous drone-type robots for surveillance and logistics. The ambition is not to build a single robot, but to develop a common Physical AI platform that can be deployed across all of these form factors — a Japanese answer to NVIDIA's GR00T and DeepMind's RT-3.

Mitsubishi Electric brings formidable resources to the table. The company's Factory Automation division is one of the world's largest, and its MELFA ASSISTA collaborative robot series already serves manufacturing lines across Asia. Equally important is Mitsubishi's motion-control technology portfolio — servo motors, controllers, and precision actuators that form the hardware backbone of industrial robotics. By layering AI on top of decades of precision motion expertise, Mitsubishi is playing to a strength that pure software companies cannot easily replicate.

Chiba Institute's Unique Contribution

Chiba Institute of Technology is not a household name, but in robotics circles it carries serious weight. The institute has deep experience deploying robots in extreme environments — including the Fukushima Daiichi nuclear plant decommissioning effort, where its robots navigated radiation-contaminated zones that were inaccessible to humans. That experience, translating laboratory research into machines that work under real-world conditions where failure is not an option, is precisely the kind of know-how that Physical AI needs to move beyond demos and into deployment.

The institute's Future Robotics Technology Center (fuRo) has also developed a range of specialized platforms, from search-and-rescue crawlers to humanoid torso systems for teleoperation research. The partnership with Mitsubishi gives these research platforms a path to commercial scale, while giving Mitsubishi access to field-tested robotic architectures and deployment expertise.

Japan's Physical AI Strategy

This partnership doesn't exist in a vacuum. Japan's Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry (METI) has been quietly ramping up support for homegrown AI robotics, concerned that the country's traditional strength in industrial robotics hardware could be commoditized if American and Chinese companies dominate the AI layer. The Mitsubishi-Chiba agreement is part of a broader national push that includes Hyundai Rotem's recently announced government-backed Physical AI projects in neighboring South Korea, suggesting a coordinated East Asian response to Silicon Valley's dominance in robot foundation models.

Japan's bet is differentiated. Rather than chasing the general-purpose humanoid dream that has captured American venture capital, the Mitsubishi-Chiba platform targets practical, application-specific deployments first — disaster response, infrastructure inspection, factory automation — where Japan has a genuine lead in both hardware quality and operational experience. The humanoid ambition exists, but it is grounded in concrete near-term use cases.

Why This Matters

The global Physical AI market is projected to reach $15.24 billion by 2032, growing at a 47.2% compound annual rate. Mitsubishi Electric's entry, with its balance sheet, manufacturing scale, and existing industrial customer relationships, changes the competitive dynamics. This is not a startup raising a seed round; this is one of Japan's largest corporations signaling that Physical AI is a strategic priority. When a company with ¥5.9 trillion in market capitalization makes this kind of bet, it validates the entire category and accelerates the timeline for industrial adoption.

What's Next

The co-creation center is expected to begin operations by mid-2026. While neither party has announced specific product timelines, the three-year agreement structure suggests a target of commercial-ready Physical AI solutions by 2029. In the near term, expect to see prototype demonstrations of the multi-legged and humanoid platforms, likely focused on infrastructure inspection and factory applications where Mitsubishi already has customer relationships. The race for the robot brain is now officially a three-way contest between the US, China, and Japan — and Japan just put real money on the table.
