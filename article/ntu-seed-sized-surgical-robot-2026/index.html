A Seed-Sized Surgeon: NTU Singapore's 4.4mm Robot Packs Five Surgical Tools Into a Single Grain-Scale Body

Published May 30, 2026

At just 4.4 millimeters — smaller than a grain of rice — a new surgical robot developed at Nanyang Technological University Singapore can move, cut, grasp, deliver drugs, and generate heat, switching between these five functions in under a second. Published in Advanced Science on May 28, 2026, the work represents what may be the most functionally versatile micro-robot ever built for medical applications.

The Five-in-One Design

Led by Associate Professor Lum Guo Zhan of NTU's School of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, the research team spent over a decade developing soft magnetic micro-robots. Their latest creation integrates five distinct surgical capabilities into a single grain-scale body.

Movement and Navigation: Controlled entirely by external magnetic fields, the robot achieves six degrees of freedom — including a newly added rolling motion around its long axis that dramatically improves maneuverability through narrow, irregular anatomical spaces. Most magnetically-actuated micro-robots operate with only five DOF.

Tissue Cutting: A retractable micro-blade, normally shielded within the robot's soft silicone body, extends only in cutting mode — preventing accidental tissue damage during navigation.

Drug Release: An internal compartment opens on magnetic command, releasing therapeutic payloads precisely at target sites. The capability has direct implications for localized chemotherapy delivery.

Tissue Gripping and Storage: The robot can grasp biopsy samples and pull them into a protected internal cavity for safe retrieval from deep within the body.

Localized Heating: Embedded Fe₃O₄ nanoparticles respond to high-frequency magnetic fields, generating heat confined to a radius of just 5.78 millimeters around the robot. This enables hyperthermia-based cancer therapy that destroys malignant cells while sparing surrounding healthy tissue.

How the Reprogrammable Core Works

The robot is fabricated from soft silicone materials — PDMS and Ecoflex — embedded with two types of magnetic particles: neodymium-iron-boron microparticles for positioning and orientation, and iron oxide nanoparticles for heating. The central innovation is a reprogrammable magnetic module at the robot's core. This module can be magnetized in different directions, with each direction corresponding to a different surgical function. External magnetic pulses remagnetize the core in under one second, enabling near-instantaneous function switching.

Critically, each functional component responds only to its designated magnetic field signature. The cutting blade, drug compartment, gripper, and heating elements never interfere with one another despite sharing the same minuscule body. The team validated all five operations in chicken liver tissue and gelatin phantoms.

Cleared for the Clinic

Human cell viability exceeded 99 percent after contact with the robot's materials. The device withstands 121-degree-Celsius autoclave sterilization and retains full magnetic and mechanical stability after 100 magnetization-demagnetization cycles. These are not academic checkboxes — they are hard requirements for any device that will one day enter a human body.

Why This Breaks the Trade-Off

Existing medical micro-robots typically perform one or two functions. Adding more capabilities has historically required larger bodies or more complex external control infrastructure. NTU's approach — using a single reprogrammable magnetic core to multiplex five functions — breaks this trade-off. The robot does more while staying small enough to navigate the body's most confined spaces: blood vessels, bile ducts, the pancreatic duct, and the intricate folds of the gastrointestinal tract.

The sixth degree of freedom is itself a meaningful advance. The rolling motion lets the robot adjust its orientation independently of its travel direction, a critical advantage when threading through tortuous anatomy where a wrong turn could mean a perforation.

The Road to the Operating Room

The NTU team is now working to integrate real-time imaging feedback, which would allow surgeons to track the robot's position and switch functions during live procedures. Animal trials are the next milestone on the translational roadmap.

Clinical deployment is years away, but the fundamental barriers — multifunctionality, biocompatibility, sterilizability, and precise wireless control — have been addressed in a single platform for the first time. In a moment when surgical robotics is pushing simultaneously toward larger, more capable OR systems and ever-smaller invasive tools, NTU's grain-scale robot stakes out the extreme frontier of miniaturization. It makes a data-backed case that the future of surgery may enter through a needle, not an incision.
