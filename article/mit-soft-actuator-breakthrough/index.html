MIT Soft Robotics Lab Develops Artificial Muscle That Matches Human Muscle Efficiency

Published April 30, 2026

For all the progress in robot AI and perception over the past decade, the physical hardware that moves robots — actuators — has remained stubbornly inferior to biological muscle. Electric motors are efficient but rigid and heavy. Hydraulic systems are powerful but leak, require bulky pumps, and are difficult to control with precision. Pneumatic artificial muscles are compliant and safe but notoriously inefficient, typically converting less than 15% of input energy into mechanical work. Biological muscle, by contrast, achieves approximately 40-50% efficiency while being compliant, self-healing, silent, and capable of operating for decades. On April 28, 2026, a team from MIT's Computer Science and Artificial Intelligence Laboratory (CSAIL) published a paper in Science Robotics describing a new type of artificial muscle that closes this gap — achieving 49% energy efficiency in a compliant, lightweight, and potentially inexpensive form factor.

The HASEL Actuator

The technology, called a hydraulically-amplified self-healing electrostatic (HASEL) actuator, operates on a principle that sounds improbable but is elegantly simple. The actuator consists of a flexible polymer pouch filled with a liquid dielectric — essentially a vegetable-based insulating oil. Electrodes are printed on opposite sides of the pouch. When voltage is applied across the electrodes, electrostatic forces squeeze the liquid, causing it to flow and change the shape of the pouch. By arranging multiple pouches in a specific geometry, this shape change can be harnessed to produce linear contraction (like muscle), bending, or twisting — all without any rigid components, external pumps, or pressurized air supplies. The MIT team's breakthrough was threefold. First, they developed a new electrode material — a stretchable conductive polymer composite — that maintains conductivity even after millions of deformation cycles, solving the durability problem that had limited previous HASEL actuators to laboratory demonstrations. Second, they optimized the pouch geometry and the dielectric fluid's viscosity to minimize internal fluid friction, which previously wasted energy as heat. Third, they developed a compact high-voltage driver circuit (a few cubic centimeters in volume) that efficiently converts a low-voltage battery supply to the several kilovolts needed to drive the electrostatic effect, eliminating the need for bulky external high-voltage power supplies that had made previous HASEL systems impractical for untethered use.

Performance and Applications

The HASEL actuator achieves a specific power of 150 W/kg — higher than human muscle (approximately 50-100 W/kg) and in the range of small electric motors, but with the compliance and impact resistance that motors lack. The actuator operates silently (no pumps, no gears, no moving rigid parts except the flexible pouch itself) and can be manufactured using low-cost roll-to-roll printing processes similar to those used for flexible electronics. The estimated production cost at scale is approximately $2 per actuator, which would make it economically viable for high-volume applications like consumer robots. In demonstrations, the MIT team built a robotic hand with 17 HASEL-actuated degrees of freedom that could manipulate delicate objects (a ripe raspberry, a raw egg) without crushing them, pick up objects of varying shapes and sizes without pre-programmed grasp strategies, and survive impacts — including being struck with a hammer — without damage. The hand weighs approximately 400 grams including actuators and structure, compared to approximately 500 grams for an average human hand, and can be powered by a battery that fits in the forearm.

The Path to Commercialization

The MIT team has founded a startup, Actuate Robotics, to commercialize the HASEL technology, with seed funding from The Engine, MIT's tough-tech venture fund. The initial target markets are prosthetics and collaborative robots — applications where the combination of compliance, efficiency, and low cost provides the strongest competitive advantage over traditional motors and pneumatics. Actuate Robotics CEO Dr. Sarah Zhang described a 3-year roadmap: 2027 for first prosthetic hand components, 2028 for collaborative robot arms, and 2029 for consumer robot applications. The company is working with several prosthetics manufacturers on integration, and the first clinical trial of a HASEL-powered prosthetic hand is expected to begin in early 2027. If the technology delivers on its promise at scale, it could transform the physical substrate of robotics as profoundly as foundation models are transforming the software.
