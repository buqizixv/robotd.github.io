1X's NEO Goes Mass-Production: The First US Humanoid Robot Factory Is Here, and It Changes Everything

Published May 31, 2026

In early May 2026, a 58,000-square-foot factory in Hayward, California began something no building in the United States has ever done: commercial mass production of humanoid robots. The company is 1X Technologies, the robot is NEO, and the implications reach far beyond a factory opening. What just happened in that nondescript East Bay industrial park is the moment humanoid robots stopped being science projects and became products.

The scale is modest by consumer-electronics standards—10,000 units in the first year—but the entire run sold out in five days after pre-orders opened in October 2025. That number tells you more than any spec sheet could. Ten thousand households, right now, are willing to pay real money for a humanoid robot that will live in their home. Not a vacuum disc. Not an Alexa cylinder. A five-foot-six machine that walks, talks, and learns.

The Factory That Changes the Map

The Hayward facility represents something genuinely new in American manufacturing. 1X describes it as the first fully vertically integrated humanoid robot factory in the United States. Everything—motors, batteries, structural components, transmissions, soft-touch exteriors, sensors—is built in-house. There is no dependency on overseas supply chains for core components. The facility employed over 200 people at launch and was built in roughly three months after permits were secured in January 2026.

A second, larger factory is already under construction in San Carlos, California. Combined, 1X targets annual production of more than 100,000 units by the end of 2027. Those are numbers that begin to matter not just for robotics enthusiasts but for the broader labor economy. The company has also announced an enterprise track with EQT, aiming to deploy up to 10,000 NEO units across the investment firm's portfolio companies between 2026 and 2030.

The manufacturing philosophy mirrors what Tesla did for electric vehicles—vertical integration as a competitive moat. By controlling its own motor design (the proprietary Revo2 tendon-driven actuator, developed since 2015 and already produced at 17,000 units), battery packs, and even the machine-washable knit suits that cover NEO's limbs, 1X avoids the supplier bottlenecks that have plagued other hardware startups. It is an expensive bet, but the speed of the Hayward build suggests it is a bet the company's backers—including OpenAI and Tiger Global—are willing to fund aggressively.

NEO Itself: Soft, Quiet, Deliberately Unintimidating

At 5 feet 6 inches and roughly 66 pounds, NEO is lighter than most humanoid competitors. That is not an accident. The robot is designed for home coexistence, not factory-floor endurance. Its body uses 3D-lattice cushioned limbs covered in a fabric knit suit available in three colors—Tan, Gray, and Dark Brown. There are no exposed pinch points, no hard edges waiting to dent drywall or bruise a toddler. The noise level is rated at 22 decibels, quieter than a refrigerator.

Under the soft exterior, the engineering is serious. Each hand packs five fingers with 22 degrees of freedom. The onboard computing module, branded NEO Cortex, runs on an NVIDIA Jetson Thor processor, trained on the NVIDIA Isaac simulation platform. Binocular fisheye cameras at 8.85 megapixels running at 90Hz provide visual input. Beamforming microphones, an inertial measurement unit, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and 5G connectivity are all standard. The robot can lift up to 70 kilograms and carry about 25 kilograms. Battery life is approximately four hours from an 842-watt-hour pack.

But the hardware, as polished as it is, is not the story. The story is what runs on it.

The World Model: Teaching a Robot to Imagine

In January 2026, 1X unveiled something it called the 1X World Model. Most robotics announcements are incremental—slightly better grasping, marginally smoother walking. This was not incremental. The World Model is a 14-billion-parameter generative video model trained on internet-scale video data that enables NEO to learn tasks it has never seen before, with objects it has never encountered, and execute them in the physical world.

Here is how it works. A user gives NEO a natural-language prompt—"pack the lunchbox," "iron that shirt," "brush her hair." The World Model generates a video visualization of NEO completing that task, grounded in a physics-informed understanding of how objects and bodies move through space. An Inverse Dynamics Model then translates those frame-by-frame visual predictions into precise motor commands that drive NEO's joints and actuators. The robot watches itself succeed or fail, and both outcomes feed back into the training pipeline.

The training recipe is worth understanding because it reveals the architecture of the breakthrough. The backbone model was first mid-trained on 900 hours of first-person human video data—people doing household tasks from a wearable-camera perspective. Then came 70 hours of embodied fine-tuning using NEO's own sensor and motor data. The Inverse Dynamics Model was trained separately on 400 hours of unfiltered robot exploration data, including random trajectories and failed attempts. The result is a system that transfers visual understanding into physical action with no task-specific programming.

In demonstrations, NEO packed lunchboxes with unfamiliar containers, operated toilet seats it had never touched before, opened sliding doors, ironed shirts, brushed human hair, and pulled tissues from boxes. None of these tasks appeared in its training data. The system generalized. That is the word that matters. Generalization is the holy grail of robotics, and 1X just demonstrated it publicly.

$20,000 or $499 a Month: The Pricing That Resets Expectations

The economics are as disruptive as the technology. NEO costs $20,000 for an early-access purchase with priority delivery in 2026, or $499 per month on a subscription model. Those numbers are jarring in a space where most humanoid robots are six-figure research platforms that never leave the lab. Set NEO alongside a Boston Dynamics Atlas—a machine that costs well over a million dollars and has never been sold to consumers—and the gap is not merely about price. It is about philosophy.

At $20,000, NEO competes with a used Honda Civic. At $499 a month, it competes with a car lease or a premium gym membership. 1X is pricing for adoption, not for margin. The subscription model is particularly significant: it creates recurring revenue, aligns the company's incentives with continuous improvement, and lowers the barrier to entry for households that might hesitate at a five-figure upfront cost.

The first-year production run selling out in five days tells us demand exists at this price point, but it does not tell us much about the buyers. Are they robotics enthusiasts who pre-ordered on faith? Early-adopter households with disposable income? Companies testing the enterprise potential? The answer matters for what happens after the first 10,000 units ship, because the retention and satisfaction data from those early deployments will determine whether the subscription model actually works at scale.

The "Robotics Slop" Problem: Honesty as Strategy

Here is where the story gets interesting—and where 1X diverges from almost every robotics company in the public eye. CEO Bernt Bornich, who founded the company in Norway in 2014 before relocating headquarters to Palo Alto, has been unusually candid about NEO's current capabilities. He coined the term "robotics slop" to describe the rough, imperfect behavior early adopters should expect. His message, delivered through interviews with the Wall Street Journal and others, is essentially: this product is not polished, it will make mistakes, and your data is what will make it better.

This honesty is strategically brilliant and ethically complicated. During a hands-on review by Wall Street Journal columnist Joanna Stern, NEO nearly toppled over closing a dishwasher, took two minutes to fold a single shirt, and managed only two tasks autonomously during the entire session. The rest of the time, human teleoperators in VR headsets were remotely controlling the robot—a fact that was not prominently disclosed in 1X's initial marketing materials.

Teleoperation is simultaneously NEO's greatest weakness and its clearest path to capability. When a human operator remotely controls NEO to perform a task, the robot's sensors capture every joint angle, every force reading, every visual frame. That data becomes training material for the World Model. More robots in homes means more data, which means better autonomous performance. Bornich calls this a "flywheel," and he is not wrong about the mechanism. The question is whether customers will tolerate being part of the training set long enough for the flywheel to spin up.

The Camera in Your Living Room

The privacy implications are real and uncomfortable. NEO's cameras see your home. When a teleoperator is controlling the robot, a stranger in a VR headset sees your home. 1X has built in some safeguards: users can schedule teleoperation sessions, set no-go zones where the robot cannot enter, and enable face-blurring. Training videos are not accessible to employees without consent. The company argues that NEO is "safer than hiring a house cleaner" because of these controls.

Critics, including prominent tech reviewer Marques Brownlee, have been less charitable. Brownlee described NEO as "a $20,000 surveillance intern." The comparison is harsh but not entirely unfair. A robot that records your home to train its AI is fundamentally different from a vacuum that bumps into furniture with a lidar sensor. The data flows back to 1X. The training pipeline depends on it. The business model depends on the training pipeline. This is not a bug—it is the architecture.

For some households, this tradeoff will be unacceptable. For others, it will be no more intrusive than the smartphones, smart speakers, and cloud-connected doorbells already scattered throughout their homes. 1X is betting that the utility of a robot that can fold laundry, load the dishwasher, and keep an eye on elderly relatives will outweigh the privacy cost. Whether that bet pays off depends on execution—and on whether the company can avoid a single catastrophic privacy incident in those first 10,000 deployments.

The Flywheel and Why It Might Actually Work

Skepticism about the self-improving flywheel is warranted. Tech history is littered with products that promised to get better over time and never did. But there is a structural difference here that deserves attention. Most "self-improving" products rely on software updates written by engineers analyzing aggregate usage data—an indirect, slow feedback loop. NEO's flywheel is direct: the robot attempts a task, the attempt is recorded from first-person perspective, the recording becomes training data, and the model improves. No human engineer needs to label the data or write a new control policy.

The January 2026 World Model announcement demonstrated that this mechanism already works in controlled settings. The key unknown is whether it continues to work as the diversity of home environments explodes from dozens of test houses to thousands of real ones. Kitchen layouts vary. Floor surfaces vary. Lighting conditions vary. The objects people own vary in ways that no training dataset can fully anticipate. If the World Model's generalization holds up under that combinatorial explosion, 1X has something genuinely transformative. If it does not, NEO becomes an expensive telepresence device that occasionally manages to fold a towel.

The Humanoid Race: Where 1X Fits

1X is not alone in the humanoid market, but its positioning is distinct. Tesla's Optimus is being developed for factory work first, with Elon Musk describing ambitions for home use eventually—but no consumer pricing or timeline has been announced. Figure AI, valued at roughly $40 billion after its partnership with BMW, is focused entirely on industrial deployment. Chinese competitors like Unitree, Zhiyuan, and EngineAI are racing toward mass production but remain primarily in the Chinese market, with export strategies still nascent.

1X alone has committed to the consumer home market as its primary target, with a real product, a real price, and a real factory. That focus narrows the technical challenges—a home robot needs to be safe, quiet, and general-purpose rather than fast, heavy-duty, and task-specific—but it also amplifies the consumer-experience challenges. Industrial robots can be janky as long as they hit throughput targets. Home robots cannot. They share space with children, pets, and adults who did not sign up for a robotics beta test.

The company's Norwegian origins, OpenAI backing, and California manufacturing base give it a transatlantic identity that helps with regulatory navigation and talent recruitment. The Hayward factory's vertical integration insulates it from the trade disruptions that could slow Chinese competitors entering the US market. And the subscription pricing model, if it achieves scale, creates a financial moat that competitors selling only hardware cannot easily match.

What Just Changed

The opening of the Hayward factory matters because it marks the transition from humanoid robots as demonstration projects to humanoid robots as manufactured goods. That transition has been predicted, promised, and postponed for years. It actually happened in May 2026. The robots are being built. They are being sold. They will be in people's homes.

Whether NEO succeeds as a product—whether it reliably folds laundry, whether people renew their $499 monthly subscriptions, whether the flywheel actually spins—is still an open question. But the factory itself is an answer to a different question, one the industry has been asking for a decade: can humanoid robots be manufactured at scale, in the United States, at a consumer price point? The Hayward factory says yes. Everything else is execution.
