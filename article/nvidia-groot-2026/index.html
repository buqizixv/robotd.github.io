NVIDIA GR00T Goes Public: The Generalist Robot AI Platform Now Open to Developers Worldwide

Published April 8, 2026

When NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang announced Project GR00T at GTC 2024, it sounded like a moonshot: a generalist foundation model for humanoid robots, trained on a massive simulation infrastructure, that would enable any robot to understand natural language and learn new skills by watching humans. Two years later, GR00T has evolved from a research project into a shipping product. On April 5, 2026, NVIDIA opened the GR00T platform to all developers, transforming it from an exclusive partnership with select robot manufacturers into a publicly available cloud service. The platform includes pre-trained foundation models, a simulation environment for fine-tuning, and deployment tools for embedding the trained models onto robot hardware — all running on NVIDIA's DGX Cloud infrastructure.

What GR00T Includes

The GR00T platform comprises four main components. First, the GR00T Foundation Model: a set of pre-trained vision-language-action models similar in concept to DeepMind's RT-3, but optimized to run on NVIDIA's Jetson Thor edge computing platform. The models come in three sizes — Nano (1.5B parameters, for simple pick-and-place tasks), Pro (8B parameters, for general manipulation), and Max (35B parameters, for complex multi-step reasoning). Second, GR00T Sim: a massively parallel simulation environment built on NVIDIA Isaac Sim that can run thousands of robot training scenarios simultaneously. Developers describe tasks in natural language ('teach the robot to fold laundry'), and GR00T Sim procedurally generates diverse training environments with varying lighting, clutter, and object placements. Third, GR00T Studio: a browser-based interface for monitoring training runs, visualizing robot behavior, and comparing model checkpoints. Fourth, GR00T Deploy: a set of tools for quantizing, compiling, and deploying trained models onto physical robots, with support for major robot platforms including those from Figure AI, Agility Robotics, 1X Technologies, and Universal Robots.

Training Any Robot for Any Task

The GR00T workflow is designed to be accessible to engineers without PhD-level machine learning expertise. A developer starts by defining a task description and uploading a 3D model of the robot if it is not already in NVIDIA's library. The platform automatically generates a simulation environment and begins training the model using a combination of reinforcement learning (trial and error in simulation) and imitation learning (mimicking human demonstrations). The developer can optionally provide video demonstrations of the task, which GR00T uses to seed the imitation learning process and significantly accelerate training. Once the model achieves satisfactory performance in simulation, it can be deployed to physical hardware with one click. The platform provides tools for sim-to-real transfer — the process of adapting a model trained in simulation to work on a physical robot, which has historically been one of the hardest challenges in robotics. NVIDIA's approach uses domain randomization (varying the visual and physical parameters of the simulation during training so the model learns to be robust) combined with a small amount of real-world fine-tuning data.

Pricing and Access

GR00T is priced on a consumption basis. Training costs approximately $2-5 per GPU-hour depending on model size, and a typical manipulation task requires 50-200 GPU-hours to train — putting the cost of training a single robot skill in the range of $100 to $1,000. Inference on the physical robot runs on the Jetson Thor edge computer (priced at approximately $500 per unit) and does not require cloud connectivity for operation. NVIDIA is offering a free tier with 100 GPU-hours of training credit and access to the Nano model, making it possible for students, researchers, and hobbyists to experiment without upfront cost. The paid tiers scale to enterprise deployments with dedicated GPU clusters and priority support. Early access partners, including 1X Technologies and Apptronik, have reported that GR00T reduces the time to develop a new robot skill from months to days. 1X Technologies CEO Bernt Bornich stated that his team used GR00T to teach the NEO humanoid to operate a coffee machine — a task that previously required six months of engineering — in under two weeks.

Strategic Context

NVIDIA's move to open GR00T is strategically significant. The company clearly sees robot AI as the next major computing platform after autonomous vehicles, and it is positioning its hardware (Jetson Thor, DGX Cloud) and software (GR00T, Isaac Sim, Omniverse) as the default stack for the emerging humanoid robot industry. By providing the AI infrastructure, NVIDIA captures value regardless of which robot manufacturer wins — analogous to its strategy in AI more broadly, where it provides the GPUs that train models from OpenAI, Google, Meta, and everyone else. The timing is also notable. GR00T's public launch comes just days before DeepMind's RT-3 paper and weeks after Figure AI's production deployment at BMW. The humanoid robot industry is accelerating rapidly, and the AI platform layer — the 'operating system for robots' — is emerging as a critical battleground. Whether GR00T, RT-3, or a yet-to-be-announced competitor becomes the dominant platform will be one of the defining technology stories of the next five years.
