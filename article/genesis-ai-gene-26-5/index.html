Genesis AI Emerges From Stealth With $105M and a Robot Brain That Cooks, Plays Piano, and Cracks Eggs

Published May 26, 2026

While the robotics industry has made extraordinary strides in locomotion and navigation, one frontier has remained stubbornly out of reach: human-level dexterity. Robots can walk, run, and even backflip, but ask one to crack an egg, tie a knot, or pipette a liquid into a test tube, and you'll quickly see the limits of current technology. Genesis AI, a San Carlos and Paris-based startup that just emerged from stealth, believes it has cracked that problem — and it brought $105 million in seed funding and a robot that cooks breakfast to prove it.

The Brain Behind the Hands

At the core of Genesis AI's platform is GENE-26.5, a vision-language-action (VLA) foundation model purpose-built for physical manipulation. Unlike general-purpose large language models that have been retrofitted for robotics, GENE-26.5 was trained from the ground up on manipulation data. It takes in visual input from cameras, interprets natural language task descriptions, and outputs low-level motor commands — all within a single end-to-end neural network.

The model is designed to generalize: train on a set of manipulation tasks, and it can adapt to new objects, new environments, and even new robot form factors without per-task fine-tuning. In one striking demonstration, the same GENE-26.5 model controlled robots performing wildly different tasks — from chopping tomatoes to playing a complex piano composition at high speed — with no task-specific retraining.

The Breakfast Test

Genesis AI's flagship demo is one of the most complex autonomous manipulation sequences ever shown: a bimanual humanoid robot cooking a 20-step breakfast meal. The robot chops vegetables, cracks an egg one-handed into a pan, scrambles it, pours a smoothie, and serves the meal. All of this was shown at 1× speed with no human intervention. CEO Zhou Xian candidly admitted the egg-cracking success rate sits around 50–60%, but the fact that it works at all — one-handed, autonomously — represents a leap forward from anything previously demonstrated.

The cooking demo isn't just theatrics. Cooking is a uniquely difficult robotics problem because it combines so many manipulation primitives: grasping irregular objects, applying variable force, coordinating two hands, handling liquids, and adapting to visual feedback in real time. If a robot can cook, it can probably handle a large fraction of industrial assembly, lab work, and home assistance tasks.

Custom Hardware: 54 Degrees of Freedom

Genesis AI didn't just build software. The company developed a proprietary dexterous robotic hand with 20 degrees of freedom per hand, creating a bimanual system with 54 total DOF when combined with the arms. The hand is human-scale, designed to operate in environments built for human hands. Genesis partnered with Chinese firm Wuji Tech to manufacture the hardware.

Equally important is the data-collection side. Genesis built a lightweight teleoperation glove covered in tactile-sensing electronic skin. The glove achieves a 1:1:1 mapping between human hand, glove sensor data, and robot hand kinematics — meaning data collected from a human wearing the glove translates directly into training data for the robot. Genesis claims the glove is roughly 100 times cheaper than conventional teleoperation hardware and collects data five times more efficiently. The vision is to deploy these gloves in real workplaces — factories, labs, kitchens — where workers wear them during their normal shifts, generating vast amounts of real-world manipulation data.

A Simulator 430,000× Faster Than Reality

Real-world robot data is expensive and slow to collect. Genesis addresses this with a hyper-realistic physics simulator that runs 430,000 times faster than real time. The simulator closes the notorious "sim-to-real gap" — the phenomenon where policies trained in simulation fail when transferred to physical robots — by modeling physics at extremely high fidelity, including contact forces, friction, and material deformation. This means the model can practice the equivalent of years of manipulation experience in a matter of hours.

The data engine pulls from three sources: glove teleoperation data, egocentric video from wearable cameras, and publicly available internet video of humans performing tasks. By combining real-world data with massively accelerated simulation, Genesis aims to build a data flywheel where every real-world deployment makes the model smarter, which in turn attracts more deployments.

The Team and the Money

Genesis AI was co-founded by CEO Zhou Xian and President Théophile Gervet, a former researcher at Mistral AI. The $105 million seed round — unusually large even by today's AI standards — was led by Eclipse and Khosla Ventures, with participation from Bpifrance, former Google CEO Eric Schmidt, and French telecom billionaire Xavier Niel. The investor list suggests a transatlantic strategy: engineering talent from Europe, venture capital and market access from Silicon Valley.

Competitive Landscape

Genesis enters a rapidly crowding field. Skild AI and Physical Intelligence are both building general-purpose robot foundation models. Kyber Labs is taking a hardware-first approach with novel actuator designs. In China, Linkerbot is reportedly targeting a $6 billion valuation in the dexterous hand space. DeepMind's RT-3 and NVIDIA's GR00T have already established themselves as major platforms for robot AI.

Genesis differentiates by going full-stack: the foundation model, the data engine, the simulator, and the hand hardware are all developed in-house. This vertical integration, if executed well, could yield tighter model-hardware co-optimization than competitors who focus on one layer of the stack. But it also means Genesis must win on multiple fronts simultaneously — a difficult proposition for any startup, even one with $105 million.

What's Next

Genesis has not announced a commercial deployment timeline or pricing. The demos, while impressive, have not been independently validated or peer-reviewed. The egg-cracking success rate of 50–60% is a reminder that these capabilities are still in early days. Durability of the custom hands under continuous industrial use remains unproven. And the real test — whether GENE-26.5 can generalize to completely novel tasks in unstructured environments — has yet to be conducted at scale.

Still, the debut of GENE-26.5 marks a significant moment in robotics. For years, the industry has been waiting for the "GPT moment" for robot manipulation — a single model that demonstrates a qualitative leap in capability and suggests a path toward general-purpose dexterity. Whether Genesis AI delivers on that promise remains to be seen, but the breakfast is on the table, and it looks remarkably convincing.
