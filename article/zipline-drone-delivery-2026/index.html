Zipline Expands Drone Delivery to 50 US Cities, Launches Next-Gen Platform 3

Published March 28, 2026

Zipline, the drone delivery company that built its reputation delivering blood and medical supplies in Rwanda and Ghana, is bringing its technology home to America at unprecedented scale. On March 25, 2026, Zipline announced plans to expand its US operations to 50 metropolitan areas by the end of 2026, backed by a new FAA regulatory framework for beyond-visual-line-of-sight (BVLOS) drone operations. The expansion will be powered by Platform 3, Zipline's next-generation delivery drone, which the company unveiled alongside the expansion announcement. Platform 3 represents a significant engineering leap over Zipline's previous systems — faster, quieter, more capable — and is designed specifically for the suburban and urban delivery markets that represent the bulk of US e-commerce volume.

Platform 3 Technical Specifications

Platform 3 is a hybrid VTOL (vertical takeoff and landing) aircraft with a distinctive design. It takes off and lands vertically using four electric rotors, then transitions to fixed-wing forward flight for the cruise portion of the journey. This configuration gives it the operational flexibility of a quadcopter (no runway required, can operate from a small launch pad in a parking lot or rooftop) with the range and speed of a fixed-wing aircraft. The specifications are impressive: 30-mile operational radius, cruise speed of 80 mph, payload capacity of 8 pounds (approximately 95% of e-commerce packages fall within this weight limit), and noise levels below 55 decibels at 300 feet — roughly the level of a quiet conversation. The drone delivers packages using Zipline's signature approach: it descends to approximately 100 feet, lowers the package on a tether, releases it at ground level, and winches the tether back up. This method requires no landing zone and works in any open area roughly the size of two parking spaces. The entire delivery sequence, from descent to package release to departure, takes approximately 45 seconds.

Regulatory Breakthrough

The key enabler of Zipline's expansion is a new FAA regulatory framework that went into effect in February 2026, establishing standardized rules for BVLOS drone operations in populated areas. Under the new framework, drone operators that meet specific safety and reliability standards can operate without a visual observer — a requirement that previously made large-scale drone delivery economically non-viable. Zipline worked closely with the FAA during the rulemaking process, contributing data from over 2 million commercial deliveries and 60 million autonomous flight miles. The company's safety record — zero serious incidents in over a decade of operations — was instrumental in building the FAA's confidence in autonomous drone delivery. The new regulations require onboard detect-and-avoid systems, redundant communications links, geofencing that prevents flights into restricted airspace, and real-time telemetry streaming to a ground control center. Platform 3 was designed specifically to meet these requirements, incorporating a phased-array radar for aircraft detection, ADS-B receivers for tracking nearby manned aircraft, and a triple-redundant flight control system.

Economic and Environmental Case

Zipline claims that Platform 3 deliveries cost approximately $2.50 per delivery at scale — cheaper than traditional last-mile ground delivery, which averages $5-8 per package for major carriers. The cost advantage comes from eliminating the most expensive elements of last-mile logistics: the driver, the delivery vehicle, and the fuel. Electric drone delivery also offers significant environmental benefits compared to diesel delivery vans. A Platform 3 drone consumes approximately 0.3 kWh of electricity for a typical 10-mile round trip delivery — equivalent to about 2 cents of electricity at average US commercial rates, and producing zero tailpipe emissions. If Zipline captures even a small fraction of the US last-mile delivery market, the carbon reduction would be meaningful. The company estimates that shifting 5% of US last-mile deliveries to electric drones would reduce delivery-related carbon emissions by approximately 2 million metric tons per year.

Market Partners and Timeline

Zipline has secured partnerships with several major retailers and healthcare providers for the US expansion. Walmart, which has been piloting Zipline deliveries in Arkansas since 2023, will expand drone delivery to 200 stores across 15 states. Walgreens will offer prescription delivery via Zipline in 20 metropolitan areas. And a major e-commerce platform (widely reported to be Amazon, though not officially confirmed) has signed a volume agreement for supplementing its own Prime Air drone fleet with Zipline capacity during peak demand periods. The rollout will proceed in phases: 10 metro areas by June, 25 by September, and the full 50 by December 2026. The initial focus is on suburban areas with lower population density, where airspace is less congested and landing zones are more readily available. Urban deployments will follow in 2027, using rooftop and parking garage launch pads integrated into existing retail locations. If Zipline's timeline holds, 2026 will be the year that drone delivery transitions from novelty to infrastructure.
