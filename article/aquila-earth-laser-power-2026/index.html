Aquila Earth Sets Two World Records: Laser-Beamed Power Keeps a Robot Running for 24 Hours Straight

Published May 30, 2026

The dirty secret of robotics is batteries. No matter how intelligent the AI or how dexterous the gripper, every robot is ultimately a hostage to its power source. A warehouse robot stops dead when its battery drains. A delivery drone must turn back before it runs out of juice. An autonomous forklift loses an hour of productivity for every charge cycle. The entire promise of 24/7 autonomous operation runs into a hard physical constraint: you cannot run forever on a battery. Aquila Earth, a Sydney-based startup with roots in New Zealand, just demonstrated a way out. In late May 2026, the company set two world records by powering a warehouse robot continuously for 24 hours using nothing but an infrared laser beam — no battery swaps, no charging pads, no downtime. The robot traveled approximately 25 kilometers on light alone. It is, in the most literal sense, a wireless power cable made of photons.

What Actually Happened

The test took place at Aquila Earth's Sydney facility. A standard warehouse robot — the kind that moves pallets, picks orders, and navigates industrial floors — was fitted with a laser receiver module developed by the company. Across the room, a kilowatt-class infrared laser transmitter tracked the robot as it moved, beaming a steady stream of light onto the receiver. The receiver converted that light back into electricity with enough efficiency to power the robot's motors, sensors, and onboard systems continuously. For 24 hours, the robot kept moving. It covered roughly 25 kilometers — about the distance from central Sydney to the Blue Mountains. The only battery on board was a nominal backup, essentially untouched throughout the test. Two world records were set: the highest total laser power ever transferred to a moving platform, and the longest continuous duration of wireless power delivery at this scale.

「Nobody else has ever done it with that much power in power beaming, ever,」 said Ruby Jones, Aquila Earth's CEO. She is not overstating. While wireless power transmission has been demonstrated in laboratories for decades — Nikola Tesla was experimenting with it in the 1890s — taking it from a physics demonstration to a practical, safe, and commercially viable system that can power a working robot in a real environment is an engineering challenge of a different order.

How the Technology Works

The principle is conceptually straightforward. Electricity from the grid is converted into a focused infrared laser beam. That beam is aimed at a photovoltaic receiver mounted on the robot. The receiver converts the light back into electricity, which powers the robot's systems. The innovation lies in the engineering details: the beam tracking system must maintain precise alignment on a moving target across varying distances and angles; the receiver must handle high power density without overheating; and — most critically — the safety system must detect any object entering the beam path and shut off the laser instantly, faster than a human blink. Aquila Earth's safety system does exactly that. If a person, a forklift, or any other object crosses the beam, power delivery cuts within milliseconds. This is not a laboratory curiosity with safety as an afterthought — it is the core requirement for any practical deployment in a working warehouse, factory, or public space.

The broader principle — converting light into usable power — also underpins the dramatic shift in economic viability. Kilowatt-class lasers, the core component, have dropped in cost from approximately $120,000 in 2022 to around $6,000 in 2026. That is a 20-fold reduction in four years. At $120,000, laser power beaming was a military or aerospace curiosity. At $6,000, it becomes a line item on a warehouse automation budget. The cost curve has crossed the threshold from experimental to industrial.

The Competitive Landscape

Aquila Earth is not alone in pursuing wireless power transmission, but its results set a new bar. Japan's Mitsubishi Heavy Industries and NTT have transmitted 1 kilowatt over 1 kilometer at roughly 15 percent efficiency — an impressive distance feat but at a lower sustained power level than what the Sydney team demonstrated across 24 uninterrupted hours. Washington-state-based PowerLight Technologies has been working on power beaming for over 15 years, with demonstrations including powering drones and ground robots, but typically at lower power levels and shorter durations. SpaceX has proposed space-to-earth power beaming — an idea Jones dismisses as economically impractical compared to ground-based solar. What distinguishes Aquila Earth's achievement is the combination of sustained high power, continuous multi-hour duration, and real-world safety integration on a practical industrial platform — not a one-off demo, but a system that kept a working robot productive through an entire day-night cycle.

The Lightway Roadmap

Aquila Earth has laid out an ambitious four-phase product family called 「Lightway.」 Phase one, Solais, targets drone flight and perpetual robotic workflows — powering warehouse robots, delivery drones, and inspection systems without charging downtime. Phase two, Bifrost, envisions energy networks connecting Earth to low-Earth-orbit satellites. Phase three, Helios, scales power, distance, and efficiency for aerospace applications. Phase four, Indra, aims for a fully integrated optical power network spanning thousands of kilometers. The vision is audacious: a world where electricity flows as freely through the air as data does today, where the concept of 「running out of battery」 becomes obsolete for industrial machines.

In the nearer term, Aquila Earth is targeting commercial deployment by 2027 and already has partnerships with what Jones describes as 「pretty large scale」 drone companies. The team's December 2025 milestone — powering an airborne drone with a consistent 500 watts of laser power — demonstrated that the technology works in three dimensions, not just on a warehouse floor.

Why This Matters

The economics of autonomous robotics are fundamentally constrained by battery technology. A typical warehouse robot spends 30 to 40 percent of its operational life charging or waiting to charge. An autonomous mobile robot fleet of 100 units in a logistics center requires roughly 30 extra robots simply to cover the downtime of the others — a 30 percent capital expenditure penalty imposed by battery physics. A delivery drone's range is limited not by its navigation system or payload capacity but by how much energy it can carry without the battery weight eating into its useful payload. These constraints are not solved by better AI or more sophisticated sensors. They are power problems. And power problems require power solutions.

Laser power beaming does not eliminate batteries — it makes them irrelevant for operational duration. A robot with a small onboard battery for safety and short-range maneuvering can operate continuously under a laser beam, never needing to dock, never losing productive time, never requiring a human to swap a depleted pack. This transforms the unit economics of every autonomous system that currently stops when its battery runs low. For warehouse operators, it means fleets that run 24 hours a day at full capacity. For drone operators, it means flights measured in days rather than minutes. For manufacturers, it means production lines that never pause for a robot to recharge.

What's Next

Aquila Earth's 24-hour test is a milestone, not a finished product. The path to commercial deployment involves certifying the safety systems for occupied spaces — a process that will require engagement with workplace safety regulators in every target market. Efficiency must improve: every watt lost between the laser and the receiver is heat that must be managed and cost that must be borne. Range must extend beyond a single room to cover entire facilities. And the economics must pencil out against the alternative of simply buying more robots with bigger batteries.

But the direction of travel is unmistakable. Just as Wi-Fi untethered computing from Ethernet cables three decades ago, optical power beaming promises to untether robots from charging docks. The 24-hour test in Sydney is the first proof that this can be done at industrial scale, at industrial power levels, with industrial safety standards. The robot that kept moving for a full day on light alone may one day be remembered as the moment robotics finally broke free of the battery bottleneck — and began operating on truly continuous time.
