Figure 02 Humanoid Robot Enters Full Production at BMW Spartanburg Plant

Published May 18, 2026

The sight of a humanoid robot walking across a factory floor is no longer science fiction. On May 15, 2026, Figure AI and BMW jointly announced that the Figure 02 humanoid robot has completed its year-long pilot program and entered full production deployment at BMW's Spartanburg plant in South Carolina — the automaker's largest production facility worldwide. This marks the first time a general-purpose humanoid robot has been deployed at commercial scale in automotive manufacturing, and the implications for the entire robotics industry are staggering.

From Pilot to Production

BMW began testing Figure 02 units in early 2025, starting with just three robots performing basic material handling tasks. The pilot expanded to 25 units by September 2025, with the robots gradually taking on more complex operations: parts inspection, kitting, and even assisting with trim installation. According to BMW's VP of Manufacturing Innovation, Dr. Klaus Berger, the robots exceeded expectations in every metric. 'We projected a 12-to-18-month pilot phase. The Figure 02 units reached our target autonomy benchmarks in 10 months,' he said during the announcement press conference. The production deployment involves 100 units operating across two shifts, handling tasks that have historically been difficult to automate due to their variability — exactly the kind of work general-purpose humanoids are designed for.

What Makes Figure 02 Different

Unlike traditional industrial robots bolted to the floor, Figure 02 walks on two legs, stands 170 cm tall, and has two five-fingered hands capable of manipulating a wide variety of objects. It is powered by an on-board AI system trained on millions of manipulation tasks, allowing it to adapt to new objects and procedures without reprogramming. The robot's vision system uses a combination of stereo cameras and structured light sensors to build real-time 3D maps of its environment. It can navigate dynamic factory floors, avoid obstacles (including humans), and locate specific parts bins without fixed infrastructure like floor markings or beacons. The hand dexterity is particularly noteworthy — Figure 02 can handle flexible materials like wire harnesses and fabric seat covers, which have traditionally resisted automation. Each hand has 16 degrees of freedom with force sensing at the fingertips, enabling it to grip delicate components without damage while maintaining enough strength to lift 20 kg.

Safety and Workforce Integration

A critical question for any humanoid deployment is safety — both physical and economic. On the physical side, Figure AI has implemented a multi-layer safety architecture: force-limited joints that cannot exert enough torque to seriously injure a person, 360-degree proximity sensing that triggers full stops when a human enters a defined safety zone, and a remote supervision system where human operators can take control at any moment. BMW emphasized that the robots are being deployed to fill labor gaps, not replace workers. Spartanburg's plant manager Sarah Chen stated: 'We have 400 open positions we've been unable to fill for over 18 months. The Figure units address our most critical staffing shortages in logistics and material handling. Our headcount has not decreased — our throughput has increased.' This framing — robots as workforce augmentation rather than replacement — is likely to become the industry standard narrative as humanoid deployment scales.

Economic Calculus

Figure AI has not disclosed exact pricing, but industry analysts estimate the Figure 02 unit cost at approximately $150,000 per robot in volume. At BMW's stated target of 20 operational hours per day (with downtime for charging and maintenance), and assuming a three-year service life, the effective hourly cost works out to roughly $7 per hour — competitive with human labor in logistics roles, and significantly cheaper when accounting for benefits, training, and turnover. The economic case becomes even stronger when considering consistency. A Figure 02 robot doesn't call in sick, doesn't make errors due to fatigue, and can operate in environments that are ergonomically challenging for humans (overhead reaching, heavy lifting, repetitive motion). For a factory running tight margins on just-in-time logistics, the reliability and predictability of robot labor is worth as much as the direct cost savings.

Industry Implications

The BMW deployment is being watched closely by every major manufacturer. Toyota, Honda, and Tesla have all announced humanoid programs, but none have reached full production deployment. Tesla's Optimus program, which Elon Musk has called 'the product that will make Tesla a $10 trillion company,' is still in early pilot phases at Tesla's own factories. Figure AI's first-mover advantage could be significant — the data collected from 100 units operating 20 hours a day represents an enormous training corpus that will accelerate the next generation of the AI system. This data flywheel effect is well understood in autonomous driving; it appears to apply equally to humanoid robotics. The company that deploys first at scale learns fastest, and the gap between leader and followers may widen rather than narrow over time. Investors appear to agree: Figure AI's latest funding round, closed in April 2026, valued the company at $18 billion — up from $2.6 billion in early 2024.

What's Next

Figure AI and BMW have announced plans to expand the Spartanburg deployment to 250 units by the end of 2026, and to begin deployments at BMW's plants in Dingolfing, Germany and Shenyang, China in early 2027. Figure AI is also in discussions with logistics companies, retailers, and healthcare providers for non-manufacturing deployments. The company's CEO, Brett Adcock, has repeatedly stated that the goal is 'a general-purpose humanoid robot in every workplace that needs one' — a vision that seemed ambitious two years ago and now appears increasingly inevitable. The question is no longer whether humanoid robots will work alongside humans in factories, but how quickly they will arrive, and what happens when they do.
