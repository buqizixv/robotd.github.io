ABB Launches GoFa Pro: Next-Gen Collaborative Robots with AI-Powered Safety

Published April 22, 2026

ABB Robotics unveiled the GoFa Pro series on April 20, 2026, at the Hannover Messe industrial trade fair, and the centerpiece feature is a paradigm shift in robot safety. Traditional collaborative robots — cobots — achieve safety through inherent limitations: low speed, low payload, and force-limited joints. The GoFa Pro takes a different approach: it uses AI-powered vision and predictive modeling to dynamically adjust its behavior based on what is happening around it. When a human is nearby, the robot slows down or adjusts its trajectory. When the workspace is clear, it runs at full industrial speed. The result is a robot that can be both safe and fast, breaking the trade-off that has defined cobot design for the past decade.

AI-Powered Safety Architecture

The GoFa Pro's safety system is built around a 3D spatial awareness engine that fuses data from six cameras embedded in the robot arm. The system builds a real-time occupancy map of the workspace and classifies every object and person within it. Unlike conventional safety systems that use simple distance thresholds, the GoFa Pro's AI predicts human movement trajectories up to 500 milliseconds into the future. If the prediction indicates a potential collision, the robot proactively adjusts — slowing specific joints rather than executing a full emergency stop. This is a significant operational advantage. Emergency stops on traditional industrial robots require manual reset, costing minutes of downtime each time. The GoFa Pro's predictive adjustment typically resolves within seconds and the robot resumes normal speed automatically when the person moves away. The system is certified to ISO 10218 and ISO/TS 15066 safety standards for collaborative robots, achieving PL d / Cat 3 safety integrity level. ABB achieved this by running the safety AI on a dedicated, redundant processor separate from the main robot controller, ensuring that main controller failures cannot compromise the safety functions.

Performance Specifications

The GoFa Pro comes in two variants: a 5 kg payload model optimized for electronics assembly and laboratory work, and a 12 kg payload model for machine tending, packaging, and material handling. Both models have a reach of 950 mm and a maximum speed of 2.2 m/s at the tool center point when operating in full-speed mode — roughly 2.5 times faster than previous GoFa generations under collaborative conditions. The robot achieves ±0.02 mm repeatability, making it suitable for precision tasks like PCB soldering and optical component alignment. A key innovation is the integrated force-torque sensing in all six joints, which enables not only the safety functions but also advanced capabilities like surface following, peg-in-hole insertion with active compliance, and automated deburring with constant contact force. These are tasks that have traditionally required dedicated end-of-arm sensors and extensive programming; the GoFa Pro makes them available as built-in functions callable from ABB's RobotStudio development environment.

Programming and Deployment

ABB has invested heavily in making the GoFa Pro accessible to non-roboticists. The robot can be programmed through three interfaces: a traditional teach pendant for experienced automation engineers, a block-based visual programming environment for technicians, and a new 'lead-through' mode where the user physically guides the robot arm through a desired motion sequence, which the system then optimizes and repeats. The visual programming interface, called Wizard, uses a drag-and-drop metaphor familiar from consumer software. Users compose programs by connecting blocks representing sensing, motion, gripping, and logic operations. Wizard can import CAD files of the workpiece and automatically generate approach, grasp, and placement poses — a feature that ABB claims reduces programming time for new parts from hours to minutes. Early adopter Siemens has deployed 40 GoFa Pro units at its Amberg electronics plant in Germany, where they handle PCB testing and component insertion. Siemens reports a 60% reduction in deployment time compared to previous cobot generations, and importantly, zero safety incidents in the first three months of operation.

Market Context

The collaborative robot market has grown from approximately $800 million in 2020 to an estimated $4.5 billion in 2026, driven by labor shortages in manufacturing and the growing sophistication of AI-enabled automation. ABB's main competitors — Universal Robots, FANUC, and KUKA — have all announced AI-enhanced cobots in the past year, but ABB's predictive safety system represents a genuine differentiator. By solving the speed-safety trade-off, ABB opens cobot applications to higher-throughput environments that previously required fully caged industrial robots. This expands the addressable market significantly. However, the premium pricing — roughly 35% above the previous GoFa generation — may limit adoption among smaller manufacturers. ABB's response is that the total cost of ownership is lower when accounting for the elimination of safety fencing, reduced downtime from emergency stops, and faster deployment.
